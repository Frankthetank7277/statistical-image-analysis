Bandlimiting and Sampling Theory

An Interactive Walkthrough of the Nyquist-Shannon Theorem

What Is a Signal?

A signal is a function of time (or space). Audio is air pressure over time. An image is light intensity over a 2D spatial grid. In MRI, the signal is magnetization intensity distributed over space.

The Core Challenge: The physical world is continuous, but computers are discrete. Sampling theory is the bridge between them.

The Frequency Perspective

Any signal can be decomposed into a sum of sinusoids at different frequencies — this is the Fourier insight. A signal's spectrum shows which frequencies are present and how much of each.

Bandlimiting: Putting a Ceiling on Frequencies

A signal is bandlimited if its spectrum contains no frequencies above some maximum fmax. This upper boundary is called the bandwidth.

Bandlimited Signal

Frequency content stops at fmax. All energy is contained below this threshold.

Non-bandlimited Signal

Frequency content extends indefinitely. Contains arbitrarily high frequencies.

Real-world Examples:
  • Human hearing tops out around 20 kHz
  • MRI scanners have finite gradient strength limiting spatial resolution
  • Blurry photographs have attenuated high spatial frequencies

Sampling: Discretizing Continuous Signals

Sampling means measuring the signal at regular intervals separated by period T. The sampling rate is:

fs = 1 / T    (samples per second)

The Nyquist-Shannon Sampling Theorem

Central Theorem: A bandlimited signal with maximum frequency fmax can be perfectly reconstructed from its samples if and only if:
fs > 2 · fmax

The critical threshold 2 · fmax is called the Nyquist rate. The Nyquist frequency is fs / 2 — the highest frequency you can faithfully represent at a given sampling rate.

Why 2× Is the Magic Number

Sampling a sinusoid at exactly its frequency would hit the same point on every cycle. At slightly less than 2×, you can't distinguish the sinusoid from a lower-frequency one. At just above 2×, you get at least two distinct samples per cycle, which is enough to unambiguously identify the frequency and phase.

Aliasing: When Sampling Goes Wrong

Aliasing occurs when you sample below the Nyquist rate. High-frequency components "fold over" and masquerade as lower frequencies. The signal is irreversibly corrupted.

Aliasing Demonstration

Concrete Example: Sample a 900 Hz tone at 1000 samples/second. The Nyquist frequency is 500 Hz. Since 900 Hz > 500 Hz, the signal aliases. The alias appears at |900 - 1000| = 100 Hz. The 900 Hz tone sounds like 100 Hz!

Aliasing in Images

In 2D imaging (MRI, photography), aliasing creates Moiré fringes — interference patterns from undersampled grids. In MRI specifically, aliasing appears as wrap-around artifact where anatomy from outside the field of view folds into the image.

The Anti-Aliasing Solution: Low-Pass Filtering

The standard pipeline is:

Continuous signal → Low-pass filter (remove f > fs/2) → Sample at rate fs → Digital signal

Low-Pass Filter Characteristics

Brick Wall (Ideal)
-24 dB/octave
-12 dB/octave

By bandlimiting before sampling, you guarantee the Nyquist condition is met. The filter removes high-frequency content that would otherwise alias.

Reconstruction: Going Back to Continuous

If sampling was done correctly, you can reconstruct the original signal perfectly using ideal interpolation:

x(t) = Σn x[n] · sinc(fs · t - n)

Each sample is weighted by a sinc function, and summing them all recovers the original. The sinc is the ideal interpolation kernel.

In practice, perfect sinc interpolation is computationally expensive (infinite extent), so approximations like cubic or linear interpolation are used instead, at some cost to accuracy.

Summary

Concept Meaning
Bandlimited signal No frequency content above fmax
Sampling rate fs How many samples per second
Nyquist rate Minimum safe sampling rate = 2·fmax
Nyquist frequency Maximum faithfully representable frequency = fs/2
Aliasing High-frequency content folding into lower frequencies when undersampled
Anti-aliasing filter Low-pass filter applied before sampling to prevent aliasing
Core Insight: Sampling is lossless if and only if you respect the Nyquist criterion. Everything else — aliasing, reconstruction error, Gibbs ringing — flows from violating or honoring that one constraint.

Connection to MRI and DFT Work

When you compute a 2D DFT on an MRI image, you're working in the spatial frequency domain — this is literally k-space in MRI.

Determines the highest spatial frequency you sampled (Nyquist frequency in k-space)

k-Space Truncation

Not collecting all frequencies causes Gibbs ringing

Zero-Padding

In spatial domain interpolates in frequency domain, and vice versa

The sampling theorem guarantees that as long as the MRI scanner collected enough k-space lines (sampled at or above Nyquist), the image reconstruction is faithful to the true anatomy.
